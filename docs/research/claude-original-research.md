# The EXPLORE Act and disc golf on public lands: a strategic blueprint for national advocacy

**The EXPLORE Act (Public Law 118-234) creates the most significant set of federal authorities for new recreation infrastructure on BLM land in a generation — yet no disc golf organization exists to use them.** Signed by President Biden on January 4, 2025, this sweeping 83-page law authorizes mandatory recreation inventories, expanded categorical exclusions for recreation permits, volunteer-built facilities on BLM land, Good Neighbor Authority for recreation construction, and 30-year partnership agreements — all authorities that map directly onto disc golf's volunteer-driven, low-impact development model. The gap is stark: climbing has the Access Fund. Mountain biking has IMBA. Disc golf — a sport with **11,165 U.S. courses, 21.2 million annual rounds logged on UDisc, and a $425 million global market** — has zero federal lands advocacy infrastructure. Across BLM's 245 million surface acres, only **three confirmed disc golf courses** exist. This report provides the complete legislative analysis, organizational blueprint, and field-office engagement toolkit to change that.

## PART 1: THE EXPLORE ACT — LEGISLATIVE HISTORY AND PROVISIONS

### How a decade of work became the biggest recreation law since FLPMA

**H.R. 6492** was introduced on November 29, 2023, by House Natural Resources Committee Chairman **Bruce Westerman (R-AR)**, with Ranking Member **Raúl Grijalva (D-AZ)** as principal co-lead. The bill drew from over a decade of bipartisan recreation policy development and compiled dozens of individual bills into a single omnibus package. **Fifty-one bipartisan cosponsors** (27 Democrats, 24 Republicans) signed on, including Reps. Curtis (R-UT), Neguse (D-CO), Lamborn (R-CO), Barragán (D-CA), Peltola (D-AK), Dingell (D-MI), and Leger Fernandez (D-NM). The Senate companion, the America's Outdoor Recreation Act, was led by Energy and Natural Resources Committee Chairman **Joe Manchin (D-WV)** and Ranking Member **John Barrasso (R-WY)**.

The House Natural Resources Committee reported the bill with amendments on April 5, 2024 (H. Rept. 118-441). The House **passed it by voice vote on April 9, 2024**, after the Committees on Veterans' Affairs and Agriculture discharged their jurisdiction. The Senate **passed it by unanimous consent on December 19, 2024**. President Biden signed it into law on **January 4, 2025, as Public Law 118-234** (138 Stat. 2836). It was codified principally at 16 U.S.C. Chapter 103.

### Structure: three titles, no dedicated funding

The EXPLORE Act contains **three titles** (no Title IV or V exists):

- **Title I — Outdoor Recreation and Infrastructure** (Subtitles A–E, Sections 111–157): Policy declarations, recreation inventories, partnerships, broadband, public-private agreements
- **Title II — Access America** (Subtitles A–C, Sections 201–232): Accessibility, military/veterans, youth access
- **Title III — Simplifying Outdoor Access for Recreation** (Subtitles A–D, Sections 301–355): Permit modernization, NEPA streamlining, volunteer authority, Good Neighbor Authority

**Critical limitation:** The EXPLORE Act is an **authorization bill, not an appropriations bill**. It creates legal authorities and mandates but does not provide dedicated funding for most provisions. Implementation depends on annual appropriations and agency prioritization — a reality made more challenging by ongoing BLM staffing reductions.

### The ten provisions that matter most for disc golf on BLM land

The Act does not mention disc golf, frisbee golf, or flying disc sports anywhere in its text. However, its provisions for recreation infrastructure, partnerships, volunteering, and permitting map precisely onto disc golf's development model. Below are the provisions ranked by strategic importance, with section numbers and specific application to disc golf.

**1. Section 351 — Good Neighbor Authority for recreation (CRITICAL)**

This provision **expands Good Neighbor Authority** — previously limited to forest and rangeland restoration — to allow states, counties, and Indian Tribes to carry out "authorized recreation services" on BLM, NPS, and USFS lands. The statute defines these services to include **"establishing, repairing, restoring, improving, relocating, constructing, or reconstructing"** new or existing recreation infrastructure. Agreements are executed via cooperative agreements or contracts, including sole source contracts. **A county government could use this authority to construct a disc golf course on BLM land.** BLM confirmed in January 2026 congressional testimony that it is "developing guidance to support recreation-related Good Neighbor Authority agreements." This is the most direct statutory pathway to disc golf course construction on BLM land. The provision sunsets after **5 years** (January 2030), creating urgency.

**2. Section 341 — Volunteers in the National Forests and Public Lands Act (CRITICAL)**

This section **extends the 1972 Volunteers Act to BLM for the first time**. Previously, only the Forest Service could formally recruit and use volunteers. Now BLM may recruit, train, and accept volunteers for "recreation access, trail construction or maintenance, **facility construction or maintenance**, and education." Critically, **BLM may not require volunteers to obtain liability insurance.** Disc golf course construction is overwhelmingly volunteer-driven nationally. This provision explicitly authorizes volunteer disc golf course installation on BLM land and removes the insurance barrier that previously deterred volunteer projects.

**3. Section 312 — Permitting process improvements and categorical exclusions (CRITICAL)**

This section mandates that agencies **develop new or modify existing categorical exclusions (CatEx) for the issuance of special recreation permits**. A CatEx applies if the proposed use is the same as or similar to a previously authorized use and does not trigger extraordinary circumstances. The section also **prohibits "needs assessments"** as a condition of issuing SRPs (except in wilderness). BLM adopted new categorical exclusions under this provision as of early 2026, though the specific CatEx list has not been publicly released in full. For disc golf, this means tournament SRPs on BLM land should qualify for categorical exclusion from NEPA review where similar recreational events have been authorized before.

**4. Section 112 — Identifying opportunities for recreation (CRITICAL)**

Directs BLM to conduct a **mandatory comprehensive inventory and assessment** of recreation resources, developed with **public comment**. The inventory must identify areas by type of recreation, assess demand, identify **underutilized locations suitable for developing or expanding recreation**, and project future recreation needs. This creates a formal, statutory process through which disc golf organizations can request inclusion as a recognized recreation type and nominate specific BLM sites for disc golf development. **The public comment window for this inventory will be the single most important early advocacy opportunity.**

**5. Section 131 — Gateway communities**

Creates new authority for DOI to assess gateway community needs regarding housing, infrastructure, and visitation, and to provide **financial or technical assistance, cooperative agreements, or MOUs** with gateway communities. Gateway communities near BLM lands can leverage this section to develop disc golf as a visitor-attraction amenity. The provision supports public-private partnerships for recreation infrastructure development.

**6. Section 153 — Partnership agreements for recreation facilities (30-year pilot)**

Establishes a pilot program allowing states, Tribes, local governments, **nonprofits, and private entities** to improve and maintain recreational facilities on federal lands via agreements of **up to 30 years**. BLM must enter agreements in at least **5 states** within 3 years. However, the **$2 million minimum investment** threshold is significant and limits applicability to major destination-scale projects.

**7. Section 156 — Outdoor Recreation Legacy Partnership Program**

Codifies the ORLP grant program for improving recreation in **urban communities and tribal areas**, with 1:1 matching fund requirements. Urban-adjacent BLM lands near underserved communities could receive grants for disc golf course development. The emphasis on low-income communities, youth engagement, and public-private partnerships aligns with disc golf's low barrier to entry.

**8. Section 113 — Federal Interagency Council on Outdoor Recreation (FICOR)**

Codifies FICOR as a statutory body coordinating BLM, USFS, NPS, FWS, BIA, Army Corps, and NOAA on recreation management, fund expenditures, economic impact research, and **identification of non-federal partners**. This creates a formal venue for a national disc golf organization to seek recognition as a legitimate recreation activity across all federal agencies.

**9. Section 132 — Improved recreation visitation data**

Mandates a unified visitation data reporting system categorizing **multiple types of recreation activities** consistently across agencies, with pilot programs for real-time or predictive visitation data. When agencies establish recreation categories, disc golf organizations should advocate for disc golf to be listed as a recognized category. Official visitation data would dramatically strengthen future proposals.

**10. Section 311 — Special recreation permit and fee modernization**

Amends and reauthorizes the Federal Lands Recreation Enhancement Act (FLREA) for 7 years, updating SRP definitions, fee structures, and reporting. The modernized framework governs disc golf tournaments and events on BLM land. BLM implemented updated SRP processing on **February 2, 2026**, through the new RAPTOR (Recreation And Permit Tracking Online Reporting) system.

### Key precedent: Congress already mandated sport-specific BLM infrastructure

**Section 123 (Range Access)** requires BLM to establish at least one qualifying recreational shooting range in **every BLM district** deemed suitable, within 5 years, free to the public. **Section 122 (Protecting America's Rock Climbing)** codifies climbing, including fixed anchors, as an appropriate use of wilderness areas. These provisions demonstrate that Congress is willing to mandate sport-specific recreation infrastructure on BLM land — a precedent that directly supports future legislative or administrative action for disc golf.

## PART 2: DISC GOLF ON FEDERAL LANDS AND EARLY IMPLEMENTATION

### Three BLM courses across 245 million acres

Despite BLM managing more surface acreage than any other federal agency, only **three confirmed disc golf installations** exist on BLM land:

**Stewart Pond Disc Golf Course, Eugene, Oregon (BLM Northwest Oregon District).** BLM's first 18-hole disc golf course in Oregon/Washington, opened September 2016 in the West Eugene Wetlands. Built through a multi-year collaboration between the **Eugene Disc Golf Club** and BLM, with "hundreds of hours of donated volunteer time." Features concrete tee pads, dual basket positions per hole, scalable layout (9, 12, or 18 holes depending on seasonal conditions), and a kiosk with course map. Free, open dawn to dusk. **BLM actively manages this site**: on July 5, 2025, BLM implemented a 4-hour parking limit to ensure equitable access among disc golf, hiking, and birdwatching users. On September 27, 2025, BLM hosted a volunteer service project to maintain the course and parking areas.

**Three Peaks and Ironside Disc Golf Courses, Cedar City, Utah (BLM Cedar City Field Office).** Two 18-hole courses in the Three Peaks Recreation Area, built through partnership between **Southern Utah University Disc Golf Club** and BLM-Utah. Features large concrete tee pads, Innova DISCatcher baskets, course map signage at every tee pad, dual pin placements, gravel parking, and informational kiosk. High desert terrain with granite formations and pinion/juniper. Ironside plays at 6,761–7,444 feet. Free, with a donation box on site.

**Ward Mountain Recreation Area, Ely, Nevada (BLM Bristlecone Field Office).** A 14-hole course. A community proposal document reportedly served as the foundation. Limited public documentation available.

On Forest Service land, the **Shady Rest DiscGolfPark in Mammoth Lakes, California (Inyo National Forest)** stands as the landmark federal precedent. Opened November 2019, it required a **20-month approval process** (January 2018 formal request to September 2019 USFS permission). Environmental surveys were required; **zero trees were removed**. Designed by 2009 World Champion Avery Jenkins. The Town of Mammoth Lakes Parks & Recreation managed the relationship with the Forest Service while the Mammoth Lakes Disc Golf Club provided community support and subsequent fundraising for additional tee pads.

Additional disc golf courses exist on Army Corps of Engineers land (Black Butte Lake, CA; Sugarbottom, Turkey Creek, Walnut Ridge, and Big Creek in Iowa) and on USFS leased ski resort land.

### EXPLORE Act implementation as of April 2026

**Secretary's Order 3435**, issued June 25, 2025 by Secretary Doug Burgum, established the Department of the Interior's framework for implementing the EXPLORE Act. The Order ensures "strong internal management and timely compliance, meaningful stakeholder engagement and private sector coordination."

Implementation milestones achieved:
- **RAPTOR system Phase I** went live February 2, 2026, enabling electronic SRP applications under Title III reforms
- **Digital America the Beautiful passes** launched January 1, 2026 via Recreation.gov (Section 322)
- **BLM completed nationwide shooting range inventory** per Section 123 (September 2025)
- **New categorical exclusions adopted** for SRP processing (per DOI testimony, January 2026)
- **Good Neighbor Authority recreation guidance** under development (Section 351)
- **Accessible recreation opportunities** identification underway across BLM, NPS, FWS lands (Sections 214–215)
- **Thomas Heinlein**, Acting Utah State Director for BLM, testified before the House Natural Resources Subcommittee on Federal Lands on January 21, 2026, at a hearing titled "EXPLORE America250 — Celebrating One Year of the EXPLORE Act"

Implementation challenges are real. **Winter Wildlands Alliance** noted in January 2026 that "implementation is contingent on having land management agencies like the Forest Service, National Park Service, and BLM staffed and funded." Ongoing federal workforce reductions have slowed implementation timelines. BLM has acknowledged "unanticipated challenges or growing pains" with new systems.

### The disc golf industry is absent from federal policy

**No disc golf entity participates in federal recreation policy.** The PDGA is not a member of the Outdoor Recreation Roundtable (80+ members representing a $1.3 trillion industry) or Outdoor Alliance (10 member organizations). No disc golf manufacturer — Innova, Discraft, Dynamic Discs, MVP, Latitude 64, Discmania — has any visible involvement in federal recreation policy. The PDGA does not have a government affairs or public policy program. No disc golf organization submitted comments on the EXPLORE Act during its legislative development, nor participated in BLM rulemaking.

The **PDGA** (founded 1976, headquartered in Appling, Georgia) is a **501(c)(4)** social welfare organization focused on sport governance — rules, player ratings, tournament sanctioning. It operates the International Disc Golf Center on 975 acres. Its Environmental Committee runs the **Throw Green Initiative**, providing environmental stewardship best practices and small grants. The **Disc Golf Foundation** is a separate 501(c)(3) focused on college courses (Matching Baskets Program) and education, having installed 17 campus courses. Neither organization engages in federal lands policy.

### Disc golf's growth data makes a compelling federal investment case

UDisc's 2026 Growth Report provides the key metrics:

- **11,165 courses** in the United States (more than Dunkin' Donuts locations at 10,014)
- **21.2 million rounds** logged on UDisc in 2025; **32.8 million player-hours** on courses
- **1,650 new U.S. courses** built 2022–2024 (97 disc golf courses for every 1 traditional golf course)
- **89% of courses are free** to play
- **3 new courses** opened per day in 2025
- Players averaged **121 days of play per year** (academic study)
- Average round: 3,800 steps, 400 calories burned, **93 minutes outside**
- Individual consumer surplus of **~$68 per trip** (peer-reviewed travel cost model, FDR/Heckscher State Parks, NY)
- Two USDGC/CDGNC events in York County, SC generated **$4.5 million in economic activity**

The **cost-per-recreation-hour comparison** is disc golf's strongest metric for public land managers. A 9-hole disc golf course costs **$9,000–$18,000** to build (vs. $28,000–$37,500 for a single pickleball court) and can serve **432 players daily** (4 players × 9 holes × 12 hours) versus 192 for a 4-court pickleball facility. Scott's own data from Utah reinforces this: **Creekside course in Lehi delivered 59,142 recreation-hours on a ~$200,000 investment**; Dragonfly delivered 11,824 hours with near-zero city funding.

## PART 3: THE ADVOCACY LANDSCAPE AND MODELS TO EMULATE

### How IMBA became mountain biking's voice on federal lands

The **International Mountain Bicycling Association** (founded 1988, Boulder, CO) provides the clearest organizational model. IMBA was born from crisis — California mountain bike clubs facing trail closures — and immediately offered solutions rather than confrontation, codifying its "Six Rules of the Trail" as a framework for responsible use.

IMBA's federal partnership timeline shows what disciplined engagement can achieve. In **1994**, IMBA signed its first 5-year MOU with the U.S. Forest Service encouraging mountain biking across 191 million acres. In **2000**, it signed a cooperative agreement with BLM — a relationship now spanning 25+ years. In **2006**, IMBA helped develop the National Trail Classification System, which categorized mountain biking as an "activity generally acceptable on all trail classes" and separate from motorized recreation — a classification victory that opened vast new terrain. In **2010**, its Public Lands Initiative protected ~3,000 miles of trails and reduced wilderness-threatened trails by ~90%. The EXPLORE Act's **Section 121 (Biking on Long-Distance Trails)** — the BOLT Act — passed as a direct result of IMBA advocacy.

IMBA operates as a 501(c)(3) with approximately **$7 million annual revenue** (growing, with a current $20 million "Leading with Trails" capital campaign), ~248 chapters and affiliates through its **IMBA Local** program, and professional trail design/construction services. Its chapter model charges a **$15/member/year flat fee** with a $200 setup fee, providing chapters with EveryAction CRM, group tax exemption, insurance discounts (Nicholas Hill Group), grant access, and advocacy platform support. IMBA is a founding member of **Outdoor Alliance**.

### How Access Fund won legislative protection for climbing

The **Access Fund** (founded 1991, Boulder, CO) offers the most direct precedent for how a single-sport advocacy organization achieves federal legislative victories. Created because climbing areas were being closed by land managers who didn't understand the sport, Access Fund built its identity as **the only national advocacy nonprofit** for climbing on federal lands.

Its crowning achievement is **Section 122 of the EXPLORE Act** — the Protecting America's Rock Climbing (PARC) Act — which codifies climbing as an appropriate use of wilderness areas and permanently protects 50,000+ existing routes in 28 states. This victory took **decades** of sustained engagement: building a network of **150+ local climbing organizations (LCOs)**, maintaining policy staff in Washington, deploying economic impact data ($12 billion climbing industry), and mobilizing rapid responses to threats (e.g., NPS's 2023 attempt to reclassify fixed anchors as prohibited installations). Access Fund operates on approximately **$5–7 million annually**, with revenue from membership, industry partnerships (REI, Patagonia), and the Climbing Conservation Grant Program ($1,000–$4,000 grants to LCOs).

The Access Fund lesson for disc golf is unmistakable: **60% of U.S. climbing areas are on federal land, which forced Access Fund into federal advocacy.** Only ~0.03% of BLM land has disc golf — but the opportunity to expand is vastly larger than the current footprint suggests.

### Other models and the coalition ecosystem

**Trout Unlimited** (founded 1959) demonstrates the chapter model at maximum scale: ~400 chapters, ~300,000 members, ~$50–100 million annual budget, and **$8.9 million in BLM partnership agreements** for western watershed restoration. TU's revenue evolution — from $2 million with 55,000 members (1992) to approaching $100 million — came primarily from federal grant leverage, not membership alone.

**Backcountry Hunters & Anglers** (founded 2004) demonstrates rapid growth: from 7 founders around an Oregon campfire to **40,000+ members** in chapters across 48 states. BHA's success came from tapping passion for public lands access with a deliberately **nonpartisan identity** (1/3 Democrat, 1/3 Republican, 1/3 Independent) — a demographic profile that closely mirrors disc golf's player base.

**Outdoor Alliance** is the coalition that amplifies all of these voices. Its 10 member organizations (IMBA, Access Fund, American Whitewater, Winter Wildlands Alliance, American Alpine Club, Colorado Mountain Club, American Canoe Association, The Mountaineers, Surfrider Foundation) collectively represent 250,000+ committed advocates. In 2025, OA conducted **323 meetings with lawmakers** and fielded a Grasstops Collective cohort that executed a Congressional fly-in. **Disc golf is not represented.**

**Outdoor Recreation Roundtable** is the industry-side counterpart — 80+ members representing businesses, trade associations, and state outdoor recreation offices. ORR led the EXPLORE Act to passage and deployed its signature stat: the outdoor recreation economy generates **$1.3 trillion and supports 5.2 million jobs**. ORR President Jessica Wahl Turner met directly with Secretary Burgum at ORR's Technology & Innovation Summit in September 2025. **Disc golf is not represented here either.**

### The gap is disc golf's 1985 — the moment before Access Fund existed

The current disc golf federal advocacy gap is analogous to where climbing was in 1985–1990, before Access Fund was created. Specifically:

- **No federal agency relationships.** No MOU, cooperative agreement, or formal partnership exists between any disc golf organization and BLM, USFS, or NPS — versus IMBA's 25-year BLM cooperative agreement and TU's $8.9 million BLM partnership.
- **No D.C. policy presence.** No disc golf voice participated in shaping the EXPLORE Act, comments on BLM Resource Management Plans, or NEPA review processes.
- **No coalition membership.** Disc golf is invisible in the Outdoor Alliance and ORR networks that shape federal recreation policy.
- **No standardized process.** Unlike IMBA's Guide to Quality Trail Opportunities (used by BLM managers), no guidebook exists for developing disc golf courses on federal land.
- **Wrong tax structure at the national level.** PDGA's 501(c)(4) status limits foundation grant eligibility and cannot receive tax-deductible charitable donations.
- **State-level organizations work only with municipalities.** ElevateUT Disc Golf — which has done excellent work in Utah city/county engagement — does not engage with BLM field offices despite Utah being ~65% federal land.

## PART 4: BLUEPRINT FOR A NATIONAL DISC GOLF PUBLIC LANDS ORGANIZATION

### Legal structure: 501(c)(3) with fiscal sponsorship bridge

**Recommended: Incorporate as a 501(c)(3) public charity, with initial operations under fiscal sponsorship while the IRS application processes.**

A 501(c)(3) is essential, not optional, for three reasons. First, **BLM cooperating associations must maintain 501(c)(3) status** per BLM Handbook H-8362-1; loss of status automatically terminates agreements. Second, nearly all outdoor recreation grants (REI Cooperative Action Fund, NFWF, Conservation Alliance, Patagonia, LWCF pass-through) require 501(c)(3) status. Third, tax-deductible donations are critical for building a membership base. A 501(c)(4) arm can be added later for unrestricted lobbying if needed (the Sierra Club dual-structure model), but the (c)(3) must come first.

Under the IRS expenditure test (elected via Form 5768), a 501(c)(3) with a budget under $500,000 can spend up to **20% on lobbying** — more than sufficient for a startup's advocacy needs.

**Fiscal sponsorship bridge (Phase 0, months 1–12):** Partner with the **Community Foundation of Utah** or **Tides Center** (7–9% administrative fee) for immediate fundraising and grant eligibility while the IRS processes Form 1023 (3–6 months typical, up to 12). You have 27 months from incorporation to file and receive retroactive tax-exempt status.

### Mission, vision, and five core programs

**Suggested name options:** Public Lands Disc Golf Alliance (PLDGA) • American Disc Golf Conservation (ADGC) • Chains on Public Lands • Disc Golf Access Alliance • National Disc Golf Lands Trust

**Branding direction:** Pair credibility-signaling language ("Alliance," "Conservation," "Access") with disc golf identity. Avoid names that could be confused with PDGA. Use imagery that combines iconic western landscapes (red rock, sagebrush, ponderosa) with disc golf baskets — visually claiming the public lands identity the way Access Fund's photography claims rock faces.

**Mission:** To expand and protect disc golf access on America's public lands through federal partnerships, community-driven course development, environmental stewardship, and policy advocacy.

**Vision:** Every American lives within reach of a free disc golf course on well-managed public land.

**Five core program areas:**

1. **Federal Lands Partnerships** — Build cooperative agreements with BLM field offices; develop MOUs with USFS ranger districts; serve as the recognized national partner for disc golf on federal land
2. **Course Development** — Identify, propose, fund, design, and volunteer-build disc golf courses on BLM and USFS land using EXPLORE Act authorities (Sections 341, 351, 153)
3. **Policy & Advocacy** — Participate in FICOR, NEPA comment processes, RMP revisions, and legislative development; join Outdoor Alliance and ORR; advocate for disc golf inclusion in recreation inventories (Section 112)
4. **Stewardship & Conservation** — Create an "Adopt-a-Course" federal lands stewardship program; develop environmental best practices for BLM terrain types; align with Leave No Trace principles
5. **Chapter Support & Community** — Affiliate state-level disc golf nonprofits; provide BLM engagement training, templates, insurance, and advocacy tools

### Component 4: Talking points addressing common BLM concerns

**"What about erosion and vegetation damage?"**
Disc golf's primary environmental impacts — soil compaction at tee areas and around baskets, vegetation trampling — are concentrated and manageable. Standard mitigation includes concrete tee pads (eliminating tee-area erosion), dual pin placements (distributing basket-area wear across 2+ locations per hole), mulching around high-traffic zones, and seasonal course modifications (Stewart Pond in Eugene reduces to 9 holes during wet conditions). Unlike ball golf, disc golf requires **no irrigation, no chemical fertilizers or pesticides, and no mowing**. A peer-reviewed study documents that 97% of disc golfers are willing to modify behavior to offset environmental impacts. The PDGA Throw Green Initiative provides established environmental best practices.

**"What about wildlife disturbance?"**
Research documents wildlife displacement (not elimination) in the immediate course area, with animals utilizing adjacent undisturbed habitat. Disc golf is a **quiet, non-motorized activity** with no projectile hazards, chemical use, or loud equipment. Seasonal closures for nesting periods are standard practice and easily implemented by temporarily removing specific baskets. Courses are designed around existing habitat features, not through them. Bear Mountain DGC in Colorado was specifically designed as wildfire prevention infrastructure, creating a half-mile defensible fire line between a neighborhood and national forest.

**"Who maintains it? We don't have staff for this."**
The partnership model is explicitly designed so that **BLM provides zero ongoing maintenance labor**. The partner organization commits to multi-year maintenance via volunteer work days (typically 4–8 per year), covering basket repair, sign replacement, mulching, litter removal, and erosion monitoring. Disc golf's infrastructure is inherently low-maintenance: metal baskets last 10–15 years, concrete tee pads are permanent, and courses require no mowing, watering, or chemical treatment. Stewart Pond DGC (BLM Oregon) has operated under this volunteer maintenance model since 2016. The EXPLORE Act Section 341 now provides explicit statutory authority for BLM volunteer programs for facility maintenance.

**"Is this consistent with our land use plan?"**
Any site proposed for disc golf development must be consistent with the existing Resource Management Plan. We will only propose sites within areas designated for recreation (SRMAs or ERMAs where recreation is an authorized use). If a site is not currently designated, we will work with the field office to determine whether a plan amendment is appropriate or identify alternative sites that are already authorized.

**"What about user conflicts with hikers, wildlife viewers, or other recreationists?"**
Disc golf is compatible with multi-use recreation areas. At Stewart Pond (BLM Oregon), disc golfers share the West Eugene Wetlands with hikers, picnickers, and birdwatchers — BLM's 2025 parking management action specifically addressed equitable access among all user groups. Course design routes fairways away from primary hiking trails. Disc golf's low noise profile and individual/small-group format generate minimal conflict with quiet-use recreationists. Signage educates disc golfers to yield to other trail users.

**"What's the liability exposure for BLM?"**
The federal government is self-insured on federal land. Disc golf carries inherently low injury risk — no motorized equipment, no projectile hazards to bystanders when courses are properly designed, no elevation exposure. The partner organization carries general liability insurance naming BLM as additional insured. Under EXPLORE Act Section 341, BLM volunteers are covered by the Federal Tort Claims Act and may not be required to carry personal liability insurance. PDGA-sanctioned events carry tour insurance with Certificates of Insurance available for $50.

**"We've never done disc golf. We don't know what it is."**
Disc golf is the fastest-growing outdoor recreation activity in America, with 11,165 U.S. courses and 21.2 million annual recorded rounds. It uses the same basic principle as ball golf — complete each hole in the fewest throws — but players throw flying discs toward elevated metal basket targets. Courses use natural terrain and vegetation with minimal infrastructure (18 metal baskets + 18 tee pads + signage). BLM already manages three disc golf courses: Stewart Pond (OR), Three Peaks/Ironside (UT), and Ward Mountain (NV). We can arrange a site visit or demonstration.
