## Five highest-leverage actions in the first 180 days

### 1. Submit public comments on the Section 112 recreation inventory
The moment BLM opens the comment period. This is the one-time opportunity to get disc golf listed as a recognized recreation type in the first-ever comprehensive federal recreation resource inventory. Missing this window could set back recognition by a decade.

### 2. Build a Cedar City pilot packet
Map one disc golf concept to Title I inventory language, Title II accessibility language, and Title III event/permitting language, with a site map, stewardship plan, and a one-page ask. Start where BLM already has disc golf credibility.

### 3. Pursue a Good Neighbor Authority pilot project
Through a county government before the Section 351 sunset in January 2030. Target counties with active disc golf communities adjacent to significant BLM acreage:
- Washington County, UT
- Deschutes County, OR
- Coconino County, AZ

One successful GNA-built course creates the template for dozens.

### 4. Apply for Outdoor Alliance membership or partnership status
Until disc golf has a seat at the coalition table, it remains invisible during the policy decisions that shape BLM recreation priorities, budgets, and rulemaking. Access Fund's founding executive sat on Outdoor Alliance's first board — that kind of embedded relationship is the goal.

### 5. Secure fiscal infrastructure immediately
The Conservation Alliance inquiry window, Patagonia deadlines, and REI cycles are all near-term. Every week without grant-ready structure is a week of missed funding.
