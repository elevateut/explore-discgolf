# Overview

**The EXPLORE Act (Public Law 118-234) is not a disc-golf-specific statute. It is a public-lands recreation law that gives BLM new tools around inventories, accessibility, permitting, gateway communities, visitor data, and partnerships. Those tools fit disc golf unusually well — but the honest framing is that disc golf is one of many recreation uses that can leverage these authorities, not that Congress created them for disc golf.**

Signed by President Biden on January 4, 2025, this 83-page law authorizes mandatory recreation inventories, expanded categorical exclusions for recreation permits, volunteer-built facilities on BLM land, Good Neighbor Authority for recreation construction, and 30-year partnership agreements. Disc golf — a sport with **11,165 U.S. courses, 21.2 million annual rounds logged on UDisc, and a $425 million global market** — has essentially zero federal lands advocacy infrastructure. Across BLM's 245 million surface acres, only **three confirmed disc golf courses** exist, despite disc golf already being a recognized BLM recreation activity category.

## Why disc golf fits the EXPLORE Act unusually well

Disc golf is already a recognized BLM recreation use. BLM's public "Visit" system includes DISC GOLF as an activity category, and BLM Utah/Cedar City pages currently promote disc golf at Three Peaks Recreation Area. A disc golf proposal starts from an activity the agency already recognizes on its own lands, not from a brand-new concept.

The Act's authorities map directly onto disc golf's development model:

- **Title I** gives disc golf a strong inventory and siting hook through mandatory recreation resource inventories
- **Title II** makes accessibility one of the best openings — BLM must select new accessible recreation opportunities in each region, including "any other" recreation opportunities identified with stakeholders
- **Title III** matters immediately for disc golf events through updated SRP categories and nominal-effects determinations
- **Good Neighbor Authority** and **volunteer provisions** match disc golf's overwhelmingly volunteer-driven construction and maintenance model
- **Gateway community** and **crowd-dispersion** provisions align with disc golf near towns and underutilized day-use areas

## The right framing

The smartest approach is to frame proposals as **"a low-impact day-use/accessibility/stewardship package that includes disc golf"** rather than claiming the statute expressly authorizes disc golf course construction.

The most persuasive pitch to BLM:

> "Disc golf is a low-cost, low-footprint, family-friendly, stewardship-ready recreation option that can help your office advance accessible recreation, youth and veteran engagement, underutilized day-use sites, shoulder-season visitation, and organized-group recreation management under authorities you are already implementing."

## Critical limitation

The EXPLORE Act is an **authorization bill, not an appropriations bill**. It creates legal authorities but does not provide dedicated funding. The strongest disc golf proposals will be those that arrive with volunteer capacity, maintenance commitments, local-government support, philanthropic match, or in-kind contributions already lined up.

## The gap

No disc golf entity participates in federal recreation policy. The PDGA is not a member of the Outdoor Recreation Roundtable or Outdoor Alliance. No disc golf manufacturer has any involvement in federal recreation policy. No disc golf organization submitted comments on the EXPLORE Act during its development. The current situation is analogous to where climbing was in 1985-1990 — before Access Fund existed.

This report provides the legislative analysis, organizational blueprint, and field-office engagement toolkit to change that.
