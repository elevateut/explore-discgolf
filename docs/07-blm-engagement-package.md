# BLM Field Office Engagement Package

## Design philosophy

The best packet is **not a glossy brochure. It is a decision-support package.** Every packet should have two layers:

**Layer 1: Universal core**
- 2-page memo explaining how the proposal aligns with the EXPLORE Act
- 1-page "why disc golf fits BLM" brief
- Standard stewardship and maintenance pledge
- Standard accessibility framework
- Standard youth/veteran/community-benefit sheet
- Standard funding/match template

**Layer 2: Office-specific appendix**
- Proposed site map and photos
- Parcel/location context
- Compatibility screen for nearby uses and likely conflicts
- Basic concept design and phased infrastructure list
- Letters of support
- Named local champion
- The specific ask for that office

**The packet should always make one clear ask, not five.** Examples:
- "Please meet with us to screen this site as an underutilized developed recreation node."
- "Please evaluate whether this concept could fit your accessible-recreation planning."
- "Please advise whether this organized event should begin with RAPTOR pre-consultation."
- "Please identify what land-use, NEPA, cultural, or operational issues we should address before a formal proposal."

## Component 1: Executive advocacy one-pager

**DISC GOLF ON BLM LAND: A LOW-COST, ACCESSIBLE, STEWARDSHIP-READY RECREATION OPPORTUNITY**
*Aligned with the EXPLORE Act (P.L. 118-234)*

**What the EXPLORE Act enables:**
The EXPLORE Act creates new authorities for recreation infrastructure development on BLM land. Section 112 mandates identification of underutilized lands suitable for new recreation. Sections 214-215 require selection of new accessible recreation opportunities in each BLM region — including "any other" recreation opportunities identified with stakeholders. Section 351 (Good Neighbor Authority) allows counties to carry out recreation infrastructure improvements. Section 341 extends volunteer authority to BLM for facility construction and maintenance, with no volunteer liability insurance requirement. Section 312 expands categorical exclusions for recreation permits. Section 156 provides grants for urban-adjacent recreation in underserved communities.

**Why disc golf is an ideal fit for BLM land:**
Disc golf is a low-impact, dispersed recreation activity requiring minimal ground disturbance. An 18-hole course occupies 15-25 acres with only tee pads, metal basket targets, signage, and existing natural terrain — no irrigation, no chemical treatment, no mowing. The sport has 11,165 U.S. courses and 21.2 million annual rounds. 89% of courses are free. Average round: 93 minutes outdoors, 3,800 steps, 400 calories. BLM already recognizes disc golf as a recreation activity and manages courses at Three Peaks/Ironside (UT) and Stewart Pond (OR).

**How disc golf advances BLM's EXPLORE Act priorities:**
- **Accessibility:** Adaptable for shorter loops, accessible tees/surfaces, wheelchair-friendly layouts, adaptive clinics
- **Youth and veterans:** Low-cost, low-barrier, scalable for clinics and wellness programming
- **Underutilized sites:** Activates developed recreation nodes and day-use areas with minimal additional infrastructure
- **Shoulder-season visitation:** Year-round activity that extends recreation use beyond peak months
- **Volunteer stewardship:** Construction and maintenance are overwhelmingly volunteer-driven nationally

**The cost case:**
A 9-hole course costs $9,000-$18,000 to build. Daily capacity: 432 player-slots. Annual maintenance: $1,000-$5,000 (volunteer labor). Creekside DGC (Salt Lake City, UT) delivered 59,142 recreation-hours on ~$200K investment. Individual consumer surplus: ~$68 per trip (peer-reviewed).

## Component 3: Talking points for common BLM concerns

**"What about erosion and vegetation damage?"**
Disc golf's environmental impacts — soil compaction at tees and around baskets, vegetation trampling — are concentrated and manageable. Standard mitigation: concrete tee pads, dual pin placements, mulching, seasonal modifications (Stewart Pond reduces to 9 holes during wet conditions). No irrigation, no fertilizers/pesticides, no mowing. 97% of disc golfers will modify behavior to offset impacts (peer-reviewed).

**"What about wildlife disturbance?"**
Research documents displacement (not elimination) in the immediate area. Disc golf is quiet, non-motorized, with no projectile hazards, chemical use, or loud equipment. Seasonal closures are standard (temporary basket removal during nesting). Bear Mountain DGC (CO) was designed as wildfire prevention infrastructure.

**"Who maintains it? We don't have staff."**
The model is explicitly designed so BLM provides zero ongoing maintenance labor. The partner commits to multi-year volunteer maintenance (4-8 work days/year). Metal baskets last 10-15 years. Concrete tee pads are permanent. No mowing, watering, or chemicals. Stewart Pond has operated under volunteer maintenance since 2016. Section 341 provides statutory authority for BLM volunteer programs.

**"Is this consistent with our land use plan?"**
We will only propose sites within areas designated for recreation (SRMAs or ERMAs where recreation is authorized). If not currently designated, we will work with the field office to identify alternatives or determine whether amendment is appropriate. The Act itself requires compatibility with applicable plans.

**"What about user conflicts?"**
Disc golf is compatible with multi-use areas. At Stewart Pond, disc golfers share space with hikers, picnickers, and birdwatchers. BLM's 2025 parking management specifically addressed equitable access. Course design routes fairways away from primary trails. Low noise profile minimizes conflict.

**"What's the liability exposure?"**
Federal government is self-insured on federal land. Disc golf carries inherently low injury risk. Partner carries general liability naming BLM as additional insured. Under Section 341, volunteers are covered by the Federal Tort Claims Act with no personal insurance required. PDGA events carry tour insurance.

**"We've never done disc golf."**
BLM already manages three disc golf courses and categorizes disc golf as a recognized recreation activity in its Visit system. 11,165 U.S. courses, 21.2 million annual rounds. Same principle as ball golf but with flying discs and metal basket targets. Minimal infrastructure. We can arrange a site visit or demonstration.

- NEPA compliance
- Section 106 NHPA cultural resource review

## Component 5: Chapter onboarding quick-start guide

**HOW TO BECOME AN AFFILIATED CHAPTER**

**What affiliation means:**
Your local disc golf club or nonprofit becomes an affiliated partner, gaining access to federal advocacy infrastructure, BLM engagement tools, insurance programs, and national visibility — while retaining your own governance, mission, and local programs. We are not competing with you. We are providing the federal-level capacity that no local organization can build alone.

**Affiliation requirements:**
1. Incorporated as a nonprofit (501(c)(3) preferred; state-level minimum)
2. Active disc golf programming
3. Signed MOU with national organization
4. Annual report on activities and BLM engagement
5. Alignment with Leave No Trace and stewardship principles

**What you receive:**
- BLM field office engagement toolkit (this package)
- Cooperative agreement templates and NEPA guidance
- Insurance program access (general liability at group rates)
- CRM/advocacy platform access
- Co-branding rights for federal advocacy materials
- Grant writing support for BLM-related projects
- GIS maps of BLM opportunity sites in your state
- Invitations to national advocacy events and Congressional fly-ins

**What you contribute:**
- Local BLM field office relationship-building
- Volunteer labor for course installation and maintenance
- Local membership recruitment
- Annual affiliate dues: $250/year (waived first year)
- Participation in national advocacy campaigns when called upon
