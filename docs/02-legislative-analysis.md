# Legislative Analysis: The EXPLORE Act

## Legislative history

**H.R. 6492** was introduced on November 29, 2023, by House Natural Resources Committee Chairman **Bruce Westerman (R-AR)**, with Ranking Member **Raul Grijalva (D-AZ)** as principal co-lead. The bill compiled dozens of individual bills into a single omnibus package with **51 bipartisan cosponsors** (27 Democrats, 24 Republicans), including Reps. Curtis (R-UT), Neguse (D-CO), Lamborn (R-CO), Barragan (D-CA), Peltola (D-AK), Dingell (D-MI), and Leger Fernandez (D-NM). The Senate companion, the America's Outdoor Recreation Act, was led by Energy and Natural Resources Committee Chairman **Joe Manchin (D-WV)** and Ranking Member **John Barrasso (R-WY)**.

The House Natural Resources Committee reported the bill with amendments on April 5, 2024 (H. Rept. 118-441). The House **passed it by voice vote on April 9, 2024**. The Senate **passed it by unanimous consent on December 19, 2024**. President Biden signed it into law on **January 4, 2025, as Public Law 118-234** (138 Stat. 2836), codified principally at 16 U.S.C. Chapter 103.

## Structure: three titles, no dedicated funding

- **Title I — Outdoor Recreation and Infrastructure** (Subtitles A-E, Sections 111-157): Policy declarations, recreation inventories, partnerships, broadband, public-private agreements
- **Title II — Access America** (Subtitles A-C, Sections 201-232): Accessibility, military/veterans, youth access
- **Title III — Simplifying Outdoor Access for Recreation** (Subtitles A-D, Sections 301-355): Permit modernization, NEPA streamlining, volunteer authority, Good Neighbor Authority

## The ten provisions that matter most for disc golf

The Act does not mention disc golf anywhere in its text. However, its provisions map well onto disc golf's development model. The key is honest framing: disc golf fits these authorities, but advocates should not claim the statute expressly authorizes disc golf course construction.

### 1. Section 112 — Identifying opportunities for recreation (CRITICAL)

Directs BLM to conduct a **mandatory comprehensive inventory and assessment** of recreation resources, developed with **public comment**. The inventory must identify areas by type of recreation, assess demand, identify **underutilized locations suitable for developing or expanding recreation**, and project future recreation needs. The law requires agencies to identify, list, and map recreation resources by recreation type and by natural or artificial recreation infrastructure — language broad enough to encompass disc golf infrastructure (tees, baskets, kiosks, parking, wayfinding).

**This creates a formal, statutory process through which disc golf organizations can request inclusion as a recognized recreation type and nominate specific BLM sites for development. The public comment window for this inventory will be the single most important early advocacy opportunity.**

### 2. Sections 214-215 — Accessible recreation opportunities (CRITICAL)

The statute requires BLM to select **at least two new accessible recreation opportunities in each BLM region**. Those opportunities may include biking, shooting, picnic facilities, outdoor constructed features, and **"any other" recreation opportunities identified with stakeholders and consistent with the applicable land management plan**. This is the clearest textual opening for disc golf in the entire Act — specifically for adaptive or accessible disc golf: shorter loops, accessible tees and surfaces, parking, signage, practice areas, or adaptive clinics. BLM's early implementation has already focused heavily on accessibility, making this the alignment point with the highest probability of traction.

### 3. Section 351 — Good Neighbor Authority for recreation (CRITICAL)

Expands Good Neighbor Authority to allow states, counties, and Indian Tribes to carry out "authorized recreation services" on BLM, NPS, and USFS lands, including **"establishing, repairing, restoring, improving, relocating, constructing, or reconstructing"** recreation infrastructure. Agreements are executed via cooperative agreements or contracts.

**Important framing note:** Disc golf courses are not explicitly named in the Good Neighbor list of authorized services (which includes day-use areas, restrooms, parking, trails/trailheads, and access improvements). The smartest approach is to frame proposals as "a low-impact day-use/accessibility/stewardship package that includes disc golf," keeping NEPA decisions federal. **This provision sunsets after 5 years (January 2030), creating urgency.** BLM confirmed in January 2026 testimony that it is "developing guidance to support recreation-related Good Neighbor Authority agreements."

### 4. Section 341 — Volunteers in the National Forests and Public Lands Act (CRITICAL)

**Extends the 1972 Volunteers Act to BLM for the first time.** BLM may now recruit, train, and accept volunteers for "recreation access, trail construction or maintenance, **facility construction or maintenance**, and education." Critically, **BLM may not require volunteers to obtain liability insurance.** Disc golf course construction is overwhelmingly volunteer-driven nationally. This provision explicitly authorizes volunteer disc golf course installation on BLM land and removes the insurance barrier.

### 5. Section 312 — Permitting process improvements and categorical exclusions (CRITICAL)

Mandates that agencies **develop new or modify existing categorical exclusions (CatEx) for special recreation permits**. Also **prohibits "needs assessments"** as a condition of issuing SRPs (except in wilderness). BLM adopted new categorical exclusions under this provision as of early 2026. Tournament SRPs on BLM land should qualify for categorical exclusion where similar recreational events have been authorized before.

### 6. Title III — Event and tournament permitting (IMMEDIATE VALUE)

For organized group recreation activities or events, the law requires BLM to make a nominal-effects determination in certain circumstances, and in some low-impact cases BLM may conclude no permit is required. BLM's February 2, 2026 implementation says updated Title III categories now apply, and the SRP page specifically asks organizers to consider whether there will be a competition or whether they will mark a course — two questions that map almost perfectly to tournaments and temporary disc golf layouts. BLM emphasizes that requirements vary by local conditions and that applicants should coordinate early with the local BLM office or through RAPTOR.

### 7. Section 131 — Gateway communities

Creates authority for DOI to assess gateway community needs and provide **financial or technical assistance, cooperative agreements, or MOUs**. The Act tells agencies to work with local governments, nonprofits, tribes, private entities, and others to **diversify visitor experiences**, including by bolstering use at existing developed but underutilized federal locations and lesser-known nearby recreation sites. Disc golf near towns, gateway communities, trail systems, campgrounds, and day-use areas is a strong match for BLM's goal of spreading use away from overcrowded flagship destinations.

### 8. Sections 221-222 — Youth and veteran provisions

The Act requires a public strategy to increase youth recreation visits, with emphasis on underserved youth, and directs development of public-awareness materials for servicemembers and veterans plus partnerships for veteran recreation events, programs, and assistive technology. Disc golf fits this framework because it is low-cost, low-barrier, easy to scale for clinics, and adaptable for wellness-oriented programming.

### 9. Section 132 — Improved recreation visitation data

Mandates a unified visitation data reporting system categorizing **multiple types of recreation activities** consistently across agencies, with pilot programs for real-time or predictive visitation data and promotion of lesser-known sites. When agencies establish recreation categories, disc golf organizations should advocate for disc golf to be listed as a recognized category.

### 10. Section 153 — Partnership agreements for recreation facilities (30-year pilot)

Establishes a pilot program allowing states, Tribes, local governments, **nonprofits, and private entities** to improve and maintain recreational facilities on federal lands via agreements of **up to 30 years**. BLM must enter agreements in at least **5 states** within 3 years. The **$2 million minimum investment** threshold limits applicability to major destination-scale projects.

## Other provisions worth noting

**Section 113 — FICOR.** Codifies the Federal Interagency Council on Outdoor Recreation as a statutory body coordinating BLM, USFS, NPS, FWS, BIA, Army Corps, and NOAA on recreation management.

**Section 156 — ORLP.** Codifies the Outdoor Recreation Legacy Partnership grant program for urban communities and tribal areas with 1:1 matching.

**Section 311 — SRP and fee modernization.** Amends and reauthorizes FLREA for 7 years. BLM implemented updated SRP processing on February 2, 2026 through RAPTOR.

**Season-extension tools.** The Act encourages coordination on off-season use, marketing, cost-sharing, and in-kind contributions.

## Key precedent: Congress already mandated sport-specific BLM infrastructure

**Section 123 (Range Access)** requires BLM to establish at least one qualifying recreational shooting range in **every BLM district** deemed suitable, within 5 years, free to the public. **Section 122 (Protecting America's Rock Climbing)** codifies climbing as an appropriate use of wilderness areas. **Section 121 (BOLT Act)** supports biking on long-distance trails. These demonstrate Congress's willingness to mandate sport-specific recreation infrastructure — a precedent supporting future legislative or administrative action for disc golf.
