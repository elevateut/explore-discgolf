# Making the Case: Why Disc Golf Fits BLM Land

## The right pitch to BLM

The most persuasive pitch to BLM is **not** "disc golf deserves a course because it's popular." The best pitch is:

> "Disc golf is a low-cost, low-footprint, family-friendly, stewardship-ready recreation option that can help your office advance accessible recreation, youth and veteran engagement, underutilized day-use sites, shoulder-season visitation, and organized-group recreation management under authorities you are already implementing."

That framing matches the statute much better than a pure sports pitch. It aligns with BLM's early implementation emphasis on accessibility, permits, visitor information, local coordination, and recreation infrastructure.

## The best near-term disc golf concepts

- Accessible or beginner-friendly day-use layouts near existing recreation nodes
- Youth or veteran clinic series
- Event/tournament proposals that use early SRP coordination
- Retrofits or stewardship upgrades at existing BLM disc golf sites
- Projects bundled with parking, signs, restrooms, trail links, and volunteer maintenance

## The weakest pitches

Remote, high-conflict, infrastructure-heavy projects with no maintenance plan, no local support, and no explanation of compatibility with existing land-use plans or other uses. The Act itself requires compatibility with applicable plans and minimizing conflicts with existing uses.

## Cost-per-recreation-hour case

Disc golf delivers more recreation hours per dollar invested than nearly any comparable facility:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| 9-hole course build cost | $9,000-$18,000 |
| 18-hole volunteer-built course | $15,000-$25,000 |
| 18-hole professional install | $30,000-$50,000+ |
| Comparable pickleball court | $28,000-$37,500 |
| Daily capacity (9-hole) | 432 player-slots (4 players x 9 holes x 12 hrs) |
| Annual maintenance | $1,000-$5,000 (primarily volunteer labor) |
| Creekside DGC (Salt Lake City, UT) | 59,142 recreation-hours on ~$200K investment |
| Dragonfly DGC | 11,824 recreation-hours with near-zero public funding |
| Individual consumer surplus | ~$68 per trip (peer-reviewed travel cost model) |
| Aggregate annual surplus (single urban-adjacent course) | ~$6 million |
| Average round | 93 minutes outdoors, 3,800 steps, 400 calories |

## Growth data

- **11,165 courses** in the United States (more than Dunkin' Donuts at 10,014)
- **21.2 million rounds** logged on UDisc in 2025; **32.8 million player-hours**
- **1,650 new U.S. courses** built 2022-2024 (97 disc golf courses for every 1 traditional golf course)
- **89% of courses are free** to play
- **3 new courses** opened per day in 2025
- Players averaged **121 days of play per year** (academic study)
- Two USDGC/CDGNC events in York County, SC generated **$4.5 million in economic activity**

## Environmental profile

- An 18-hole course occupies **15-25 acres** with only tee pads (concrete or aggregate, 4'x8'), 18 metal basket targets, signage, and existing natural terrain
- **No irrigation, no chemical treatment, no mowing, no motorized equipment in operation**
- Courses are designed around existing vegetation and topography
- Standard mitigation: concrete tee pads (eliminating tee-area erosion), dual pin placements (distributing wear across 2+ locations per hole), mulching around high-traffic zones, seasonal course modifications
- 97% of disc golfers are willing to modify behavior to offset environmental impacts (peer-reviewed)
- Bear Mountain DGC in Colorado was specifically designed as wildfire prevention infrastructure, creating a half-mile defensible fire line

## How disc golf advances BLM's EXPLORE Act priorities

| BLM Priority | How Disc Golf Fits |
|-------------|-------------------|
| **Accessibility** (Sections 214-215) | Adaptable for shorter loops, accessible tees/surfaces, wheelchair-friendly layouts, adaptive clinics |
| **Youth engagement** (Section 221) | Low-cost, low-barrier, scalable for clinics; emphasis on underserved youth |
| **Veteran wellness** (Section 222) | Adaptable for wellness programming; low-barrier outdoor activity |
| **Underutilized sites** (Section 131) | Activates developed recreation nodes and day-use areas with minimal infrastructure |
| **Shoulder-season visitation** | Year-round activity extending recreation use beyond peak months |
| **Volunteer stewardship** (Section 341) | Construction and maintenance are overwhelmingly volunteer-driven nationally |
| **Gateway communities** (Section 131) | Visitor-attraction amenity for communities near BLM lands |
| **Crowd dispersion** (Section 132) | Draws visitors to lesser-known sites, reducing pressure on flagships |
| **Low federal cost** | Strongest proposals arrive with volunteer capacity, maintenance, and local match already lined up |
