# The EXPLORE Act and Disc Golf on Public Lands: Strategic Analysis and Action Blueprint

**The EXPLORE Act (Public Law 118-234) is not a disc-golf-specific statute. It is a public-lands recreation law that gives BLM new tools around inventories, accessibility, permitting, gateway communities, visitor data, and partnerships. Those tools fit disc golf unusually well — but the honest framing is that disc golf is one of many recreation uses that can leverage these authorities, not that Congress created them for disc golf.**

Signed by President Biden on January 4, 2025, this 83-page law authorizes mandatory recreation inventories, expanded categorical exclusions for recreation permits, volunteer-built facilities on BLM land, Good Neighbor Authority for recreation construction, and 30-year partnership agreements. Disc golf — a sport with **11,165 U.S. courses, 21.2 million annual rounds logged on UDisc, and a $425 million global market** — has essentially zero federal lands advocacy infrastructure. Across BLM's 245 million surface acres, only **three confirmed disc golf courses** exist, despite disc golf already being a recognized BLM recreation activity category. This report provides the legislative analysis, organizational blueprint, and field-office engagement toolkit to change that.

## PART 1: THE EXPLORE ACT — LEGISLATIVE HISTORY AND PROVISIONS

### How a decade of work became the biggest recreation law since FLPMA

**H.R. 6492** was introduced on November 29, 2023, by House Natural Resources Committee Chairman **Bruce Westerman (R-AR)**, with Ranking Member **Raul Grijalva (D-AZ)** as principal co-lead. The bill compiled dozens of individual bills into a single omnibus package with **51 bipartisan cosponsors** (27 Democrats, 24 Republicans), including Reps. Curtis (R-UT), Neguse (D-CO), Lamborn (R-CO), Barragan (D-CA), Peltola (D-AK), Dingell (D-MI), and Leger Fernandez (D-NM). The Senate companion, the America's Outdoor Recreation Act, was led by Energy and Natural Resources Committee Chairman **Joe Manchin (D-WV)** and Ranking Member **John Barrasso (R-WY)**.

The House Natural Resources Committee reported the bill with amendments on April 5, 2024 (H. Rept. 118-441). The House **passed it by voice vote on April 9, 2024**. The Senate **passed it by unanimous consent on December 19, 2024**. President Biden signed it into law on **January 4, 2025, as Public Law 118-234** (138 Stat. 2836), codified principally at 16 U.S.C. Chapter 103.

### Structure: three titles, no dedicated funding

The EXPLORE Act contains **three titles**:

- **Title I — Outdoor Recreation and Infrastructure** (Subtitles A-E, Sections 111-157): Policy declarations, recreation inventories, partnerships, broadband, public-private agreements
- **Title II — Access America** (Subtitles A-C, Sections 201-232): Accessibility, military/veterans, youth access
- **Title III — Simplifying Outdoor Access for Recreation** (Subtitles A-D, Sections 301-355): Permit modernization, NEPA streamlining, volunteer authority, Good Neighbor Authority

**Critical limitation:** The EXPLORE Act is an **authorization bill, not an appropriations bill**. It creates legal authorities and mandates but does not provide dedicated funding. The Act expressly says it authorizes no additional federal funds beyond appropriations. In practice, the strongest disc golf proposals will be those that arrive with volunteer capacity, maintenance commitments, local-government support, philanthropic match, or in-kind contributions already lined up.

### The ten provisions that matter most for disc golf

The Act does not mention disc golf anywhere in its text. However, its provisions map well onto disc golf's development model. The key is honest framing: disc golf fits these authorities, but advocates should not claim the statute expressly authorizes disc golf course construction. The smartest framing is usually **"a low-impact day-use/accessibility/stewardship package that includes disc golf."**

**1. Section 112 — Identifying opportunities for recreation (CRITICAL)**

Directs BLM to conduct a **mandatory comprehensive inventory and assessment** of recreation resources, developed with **public comment**. The inventory must identify areas by type of recreation, assess demand, identify **underutilized locations suitable for developing or expanding recreation**, and project future recreation needs. The law requires agencies to identify, list, and map recreation resources by recreation type and by natural or artificial recreation infrastructure — language broad enough to encompass disc golf infrastructure (tees, baskets, kiosks, parking, wayfinding).

**This creates a formal, statutory process through which disc golf organizations can request inclusion as a recognized recreation type and nominate specific BLM sites for development. The public comment window for this inventory will be the single most important early advocacy opportunity.**

**2. Sections 214-215 — Accessible recreation opportunities (CRITICAL)**

The statute requires BLM to select **at least two new accessible recreation opportunities in each BLM region**. Those opportunities may include biking, shooting, picnic facilities, outdoor constructed features, and **"any other" recreation opportunities identified with stakeholders and consistent with the applicable land management plan**. This is the clearest textual opening for disc golf in the entire Act — specifically for adaptive or accessible disc golf: shorter loops, accessible tees and surfaces, parking, signage, practice areas, or adaptive clinics. BLM's early implementation has already focused heavily on accessibility, making this the alignment point with the highest probability of traction.

**3. Section 351 — Good Neighbor Authority for recreation (CRITICAL)**

Expands Good Neighbor Authority to allow states, counties, and Indian Tribes to carry out "authorized recreation services" on BLM, NPS, and USFS lands, including **"establishing, repairing, restoring, improving, relocating, constructing, or reconstructing"** recreation infrastructure. Agreements are executed via cooperative agreements or contracts.

**Important framing note:** Disc golf courses are not explicitly named in the Good Neighbor list of authorized services (which includes day-use areas, restrooms, parking, trails/trailheads, and access improvements). The smartest approach is to frame proposals as "a low-impact day-use/accessibility/stewardship package that includes disc golf," keeping NEPA decisions federal. A county government could use this authority to construct recreation infrastructure that includes a disc golf course. **This provision sunsets after 5 years (January 2030), creating urgency.** BLM confirmed in January 2026 testimony that it is "developing guidance to support recreation-related Good Neighbor Authority agreements."

**4. Section 341 — Volunteers in the National Forests and Public Lands Act (CRITICAL)**

**Extends the 1972 Volunteers Act to BLM for the first time.** BLM may now recruit, train, and accept volunteers for "recreation access, trail construction or maintenance, **facility construction or maintenance**, and education." Critically, **BLM may not require volunteers to obtain liability insurance.** Disc golf course construction is overwhelmingly volunteer-driven nationally. This provision explicitly authorizes volunteer disc golf course installation on BLM land and removes the insurance barrier.

**5. Section 312 — Permitting process improvements and categorical exclusions (CRITICAL)**

Mandates that agencies **develop new or modify existing categorical exclusions (CatEx) for special recreation permits**. Also **prohibits "needs assessments"** as a condition of issuing SRPs (except in wilderness). BLM adopted new categorical exclusions under this provision as of early 2026. For disc golf, tournament SRPs on BLM land should qualify for categorical exclusion where similar recreational events have been authorized before.

**6. Title III — Event and tournament permitting (IMMEDIATE VALUE)**

For organized group recreation activities or events, the law requires BLM to make a nominal-effects determination in certain circumstances, and in some low-impact cases BLM may conclude no permit is required. BLM's February 2, 2026 implementation says updated Title III categories now apply, and the SRP page specifically asks organizers to consider whether there will be a competition or whether they will mark a course — two questions that map almost perfectly to tournaments and temporary disc golf layouts. BLM emphasizes that requirements vary by local conditions and that applicants should coordinate early with the local BLM office or through RAPTOR.

**7. Section 131 — Gateway communities**

Creates authority for DOI to assess gateway community needs and provide **financial or technical assistance, cooperative agreements, or MOUs**. The Act tells agencies to work with local governments, nonprofits, tribes, private entities, and others to **diversify visitor experiences**, including by bolstering use at existing developed but underutilized federal locations and lesser-known nearby recreation sites. Disc golf near towns, gateway communities, trail systems, campgrounds, and day-use areas is a strong match for BLM's goal of spreading use away from overcrowded flagship destinations.

**8. Sections 221-222 — Youth and veteran provisions**

The Act requires a public strategy to increase youth recreation visits, with emphasis on underserved youth, and directs development of public-awareness materials for servicemembers and veterans plus partnerships for veteran recreation events, programs, and assistive technology. Disc golf fits this framework because it is low-cost, low-barrier, easy to scale for clinics, and adaptable for wellness-oriented programming.

**9. Section 132 — Improved recreation visitation data**

Mandates a unified visitation data reporting system categorizing **multiple types of recreation activities** consistently across agencies, with pilot programs for real-time or predictive visitation data and promotion of lesser-known sites. When agencies establish recreation categories, disc golf organizations should advocate for disc golf to be listed as a recognized category. Official visitation data would dramatically strengthen future proposals.

**10. Section 153 — Partnership agreements for recreation facilities (30-year pilot)**

Establishes a pilot program allowing states, Tribes, local governments, **nonprofits, and private entities** to improve and maintain recreational facilities on federal lands via agreements of **up to 30 years**. BLM must enter agreements in at least **5 states** within 3 years. The **$2 million minimum investment** threshold limits applicability to major destination-scale projects.

### Other provisions worth noting

**Section 113 — FICOR.** Codifies the Federal Interagency Council on Outdoor Recreation as a statutory body coordinating BLM, USFS, NPS, FWS, BIA, Army Corps, and NOAA on recreation management. Creates a formal venue for a disc golf organization to seek recognition across all federal agencies.

**Section 156 — ORLP.** Codifies the Outdoor Recreation Legacy Partnership grant program for improving recreation in **urban communities and tribal areas** with 1:1 matching. Urban-adjacent BLM lands near underserved communities could receive grants for disc golf development.

**Section 311 — SRP and fee modernization.** Amends and reauthorizes FLREA for 7 years. BLM implemented updated SRP processing on **February 2, 2026** through RAPTOR (Recreation And Permit Tracking Online Reporting).

**Season-extension tools.** The Act encourages BLM and other agencies to coordinate with businesses, nonprofits, local governments, and others on off-season use, marketing campaigns, cost-sharing, and in-kind contributions. Disc golf is a year-round activity in many climates and fits shoulder-season programming well.

### Key precedent: Congress already mandated sport-specific BLM infrastructure

**Section 123 (Range Access)** requires BLM to establish at least one qualifying recreational shooting range in **every BLM district** deemed suitable, within 5 years, free to the public. **Section 122 (Protecting America's Rock Climbing)** codifies climbing, including fixed anchors, as an appropriate use of wilderness areas. **Section 121 (BOLT Act)** supports biking on long-distance trails. These provisions demonstrate Congress's willingness to mandate sport-specific recreation infrastructure on BLM land — a precedent that supports future legislative or administrative action for disc golf.

## PART 2: DISC GOLF ON FEDERAL LANDS — CURRENT STATE AND EARLY IMPLEMENTATION

### Disc golf is already a recognized BLM recreation use

This is an important starting point that changes the advocacy framing. **BLM's public "Visit" system already includes DISC GOLF as an activity category**, and BLM Utah/Cedar City pages currently promote disc golf at Three Peaks Recreation Area, including separate BLM pages for the Three Peaks and Ironside 18-hole courses. A disc golf proposal starts from an activity the agency already recognizes on its own lands, not from a brand-new concept.

### Three BLM courses across 245 million acres

**Stewart Pond Disc Golf Course, Eugene, Oregon (BLM Northwest Oregon District).** BLM's first 18-hole disc golf course in Oregon/Washington, opened September 2016 in the West Eugene Wetlands. Built through multi-year collaboration between the **Eugene Disc Golf Club** and BLM, with "hundreds of hours of donated volunteer time." Features concrete tee pads, dual basket positions, scalable layout (9, 12, or 18 holes depending on seasonal conditions), and a kiosk with course map. Free, open dawn to dusk. BLM actively manages this site: in July 2025 BLM implemented a 4-hour parking limit to ensure equitable access among disc golf, hiking, and birdwatching users. In September 2025, BLM hosted a volunteer service project to maintain the course.

**Three Peaks and Ironside Disc Golf Courses, Cedar City, Utah (BLM Cedar City Field Office).** Two 18-hole courses in the Three Peaks Recreation Area, built through partnership between **Southern Utah University Disc Golf Club** and BLM-Utah. Features large concrete tee pads, Innova DISCatcher baskets, course map signage at every tee pad, dual pin placements, gravel parking, and informational kiosk. High desert terrain with granite formations and pinion/juniper. Ironside plays at 6,761-7,444 feet. Free, with a donation box on site.

**Ward Mountain Recreation Area, Ely, Nevada (BLM Bristlecone Field Office).** A 14-hole course. Limited public documentation available.

**Additional federal precedent:** The **Shady Rest DiscGolfPark in Mammoth Lakes, California (Inyo National Forest)** required a 20-month approval process (January 2018 to September 2019). Environmental surveys were required; zero trees were removed. Designed by 2009 World Champion Avery Jenkins. Courses also exist on Army Corps of Engineers land and USFS leased ski resort land.

### Post-Act field office activity

**Barnes Grade Recreation Area (Applegate Field Office).** In September 2025, the Applegate Field Office issued a decision authorizing the Barnes Grade Recreation Area, including three parking areas, one day-use area, **a nine-hole disc golf course**, and related infrastructure. This is hard evidence that a BLM field office approved disc golf infrastructure after the Act became law. However, no public BLM document explicitly says Barnes Grade was approved *because of* the EXPLORE Act. The fair statement: it is evidence that disc golf is very much within the kind of recreation development BLM is advancing in the EXPLORE era, not proof that a specific EXPLORE section directly triggered that course.

### EXPLORE Act implementation as of April 2026

**Secretary's Order 3435**, issued June 25, 2025 by Secretary Doug Burgum, established DOI's framework for implementing the EXPLORE Act.

**Direct implementation milestones:**
- **Digital America the Beautiful passes** launched January 1, 2026 via Recreation.gov (Section 322)
- **RAPTOR system Phase I** went live February 2, 2026, enabling electronic SRP applications under Title III reforms
- **New categorical exclusions adopted** for SRP processing (per DOI testimony, January 2026)
- **BLM completed nationwide shooting range inventory** per Section 123 (September 2025)
- **Good Neighbor Authority recreation guidance** under development (Section 351)
- **Accessible recreation opportunities** identification underway across BLM, NPS, FWS lands (Sections 214-215)
- **Thomas Heinlein**, Acting Utah State Director for BLM, testified before the House Natural Resources Subcommittee on January 21, 2026

**Early investment and staffing:**
- BLM collaborated with the Foundation for America's Public Lands on **10 recreation and access projects** in Arizona, California, Colorado, Florida, Montana, New Mexico, Utah, and Wyoming, totaling **$1 million in direct investments and $4 million in matching funds**
- BLM expanded staff, formed a dedicated EXPLORE "core team," and began developing field-office guidance
- FY2026 budget justification explicitly says BLM is implementing Title III permitting improvements
- BLM recorded **more than 80 million recreation visits** in FY2024

**Accessibility rollout (the area with most visible early momentum):**
- BLM added enhanced accessibility descriptions to **more than 100 recreation sites** and assembled photos for **76+ sites**
- Second phase started in Utah, updating nearly 25 Utah Visit pages
- State-by-state accessibility stories published, tied explicitly to supporting the EXPLORE Act
- Highlights include Phoenix District's accessible shooting sites, Colorado's Uncompahgre Field Office adaptive bike trail, and Idaho's adaptive-ready Hang Tight/Hang Loose trail

**Field-office level reporting:**
- Northern California RAC minutes (May 2025): BLM examining each EXPLORE Act subsection, identifying policy/regulatory changes, developing implementation guidance and timelines
- Applegate reported increased work developing recreation trails
- Eagle Lake reported recreation accomplishments
- Redding reported campground openings, recreation accomplishments, planning, and outreach

**Implementation challenges are real.** Winter Wildlands Alliance noted in January 2026 that "implementation is contingent on having land management agencies like the Forest Service, National Park Service, and BLM staffed and funded." Ongoing federal workforce reductions have slowed timelines.

## PART 3: MAKING THE CASE — WHY DISC GOLF FITS BLM LAND

### The right pitch to BLM

The most persuasive pitch to BLM is **not** "disc golf deserves a course because it's popular." The best pitch is:

> "Disc golf is a low-cost, low-footprint, family-friendly, stewardship-ready recreation option that can help your office advance accessible recreation, youth and veteran engagement, underutilized day-use sites, shoulder-season visitation, and organized-group recreation management under authorities you are already implementing."

That framing matches the statute much better than a pure sports pitch. It aligns with BLM's early implementation emphasis on accessibility, permits, visitor information, local coordination, and recreation infrastructure.

### The cost-per-recreation-hour case

Disc golf delivers more recreation hours per dollar invested than nearly any comparable facility:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| 9-hole course build cost | $9,000-$18,000 |
| 18-hole volunteer-built course | $15,000-$25,000 |
| 18-hole professional install | $30,000-$50,000+ |
| Comparable pickleball court | $28,000-$37,500 |
| Daily capacity (9-hole) | 432 player-slots (4 players x 9 holes x 12 hrs) |
| Annual maintenance | $1,000-$5,000 (primarily volunteer labor) |
| Creekside DGC (Salt Lake City, UT) | 59,142 recreation-hours on ~$200K investment |
| Dragonfly DGC | 11,824 recreation-hours with near-zero public funding |
| Individual consumer surplus | ~$68 per trip (peer-reviewed) |
| Average round | 93 minutes outdoors, 3,800 steps, 400 calories |

### Growth data

- **11,165 courses** in the United States (more than Dunkin' Donuts at 10,014)
- **21.2 million rounds** logged on UDisc in 2025; **32.8 million player-hours**
- **1,650 new U.S. courses** built 2022-2024 (97 disc golf courses for every 1 traditional golf course)
- **89% of courses are free** to play
- **3 new courses** opened per day in 2025
- Players averaged **121 days of play per year**
- Two USDGC/CDGNC events in York County, SC generated **$4.5 million in economic activity**

### Environmental profile

- An 18-hole course occupies **15-25 acres** with only tee pads (concrete or aggregate, 4'x8'), 18 metal basket targets, signage, and existing natural terrain
- **No irrigation, no chemical treatment, no mowing, no motorized equipment in operation**
- Courses are designed around existing vegetation and topography
- Standard mitigation: concrete tee pads (eliminating tee-area erosion), dual pin placements (distributing wear), mulching, seasonal course modifications
- 97% of disc golfers are willing to modify behavior to offset environmental impacts (peer-reviewed)
- Bear Mountain DGC in Colorado was specifically designed as wildfire prevention infrastructure

## PART 4: THE ADVOCACY LANDSCAPE AND MODELS TO EMULATE

### The disc golf federal advocacy gap

**No disc golf entity participates in federal recreation policy.** The PDGA is not a member of the Outdoor Recreation Roundtable (80+ members, $1.3 trillion industry) or Outdoor Alliance (10 member organizations). No disc golf manufacturer has any visible involvement in federal recreation policy. The PDGA has no government affairs program. No disc golf organization submitted comments on the EXPLORE Act during its development or participated in BLM rulemaking.

The current gap is analogous to where climbing was in 1985-1990, before Access Fund existed:

- **No federal agency relationships.** No MOU, cooperative agreement, or formal partnership exists between any disc golf organization and BLM, USFS, or NPS.
- **No D.C. policy presence.** No disc golf voice participated in shaping the EXPLORE Act.
- **No coalition membership.** Disc golf is invisible in Outdoor Alliance and ORR.
- **No standardized process.** No guidebook exists for developing disc golf courses on federal land.
- **Wrong tax structure at national level.** PDGA's 501(c)(4) status limits foundation grant eligibility.
- **State organizations work only with municipalities.** ElevateUT Disc Golf does not engage with BLM field offices despite Utah being ~65% federal land.

### How IMBA became mountain biking's voice on federal lands

The **International Mountain Bicycling Association** (founded 1988, Boulder, CO) was born from crisis — California clubs facing trail closures — and immediately offered solutions rather than confrontation. In 1994, IMBA signed its first 5-year MOU with USFS. In 2000, it signed a cooperative agreement with BLM — now spanning 25+ years. In 2006, it helped develop the National Trail Classification System, classifying mountain biking as "generally acceptable on all trail classes." The EXPLORE Act's Section 121 (BOLT Act) passed as a direct result of IMBA advocacy.

IMBA operates as a 501(c)(3) with ~$7 million annual revenue (current $20 million capital campaign), ~248 chapters through its IMBA Local program. Chapter model: $15/member/year flat fee, $200 setup fee, providing CRM, group tax exemption, insurance, grant access, and advocacy platform.

### How Access Fund won legislative protection for climbing

The **Access Fund** (founded 1991, Boulder, CO) is the most direct precedent for a single-sport federal advocacy win. Its crowning achievement is Section 122 of the EXPLORE Act — the PARC Act — codifying climbing as an appropriate use of wilderness areas, permanently protecting 50,000+ routes in 28 states. This took decades: building 150+ local climbing organizations, maintaining DC policy staff, deploying economic data, and mobilizing rapid threat responses. Budget: ~$5-7 million annually.

The lesson: 60% of U.S. climbing areas are on federal land, which forced Access Fund into federal advocacy. Only ~0.03% of BLM land has disc golf — but the opportunity to expand is vastly larger than the current footprint.

### Other models

**Trout Unlimited** (founded 1959): ~400 chapters, ~300,000 members, ~$50-100M annual budget, **$8.9 million in BLM partnership agreements**. Revenue grew from $2M (1992) to ~$100M primarily through federal grant leverage, not membership alone.

**Backcountry Hunters & Anglers** (founded 2004): From 7 founders around an Oregon campfire to **40,000+ members** across 48 states. Success came from tapping public lands passion with a deliberately **nonpartisan identity** (1/3 D, 1/3 R, 1/3 I) — a demographic profile that mirrors disc golf's player base.

**Outdoor Alliance**: 10 member organizations collectively representing 250,000+ advocates. In 2025, OA conducted **323 meetings with lawmakers**. Disc golf is not represented.

**Outdoor Recreation Roundtable**: 80+ members. ORR led the EXPLORE Act to passage. President Jessica Wahl Turner met with Secretary Burgum at ORR's September 2025 summit. Disc golf is not represented.

## PART 5: ORGANIZATIONAL STRATEGY

### Use the existing 501(c)(3) — don't start from scratch

Given ElevateUT's existing nonprofit infrastructure, the strongest path is **not** creating a separate brand-new entity. The cleaner model is to run this as a **dedicated program or campaign inside the existing 501(c)(3)**, with a public-facing name and office-by-office toolkit.

**Why a 501(c)(3) is essential:**
- BLM cooperating associations must maintain 501(c)(3) status per BLM Handbook H-8362-1; loss of status terminates agreements
- Nearly all outdoor recreation grants (REI, NFWF, Conservation Alliance, Patagonia, LWCF pass-through) require 501(c)(3) status
- Tax-deductible donations are critical for building a membership base
- Under the IRS expenditure test (Form 5768), a 501(c)(3) under $500K budget can spend up to **20% on lobbying** — more than sufficient

**Only add a separate 501(c)(4) if heavy lobbying becomes central.** A c4 social welfare organization may lobby as its primary activity. That is useful only if legislative advocacy becomes a major long-term lane. Most groups should not start there.

### Operating model: hub-and-spoke campaign, not a club

- **Central hub** that translates the statute, builds packet templates, tracks evidence, and manages the public story
- **Local ambassadors**, one per BLM office or district, who tailor packets and maintain relationships
- **Technical advisory bench** for accessibility, course design, stewardship, risk, and land-use compatibility
- **Partner layer** including counties, chambers, adaptive-sports groups, youth providers, veterans groups, and local clubs

This mirrors the law's repeated emphasis on stakeholder consultation, local coordination, and leverage of non-federal resources.

### Public-facing initiative name

Create a branded initiative under the existing 501(c)(3). Suggested names: **Public Lands Disc Golf Alliance (PLDGA)** | American Disc Golf Conservation (ADGC) | Disc Golf Access Alliance | National Disc Golf Lands Trust

Branding direction: pair credibility-signaling language ("Alliance," "Conservation," "Access") with disc golf identity. Use imagery combining iconic western landscapes with disc golf baskets.

### Mission and vision

**Mission:** To expand and protect disc golf access on America's public lands through federal partnerships, community-driven course development, environmental stewardship, and policy advocacy.

**Vision:** Every American lives within reach of a free disc golf course on well-managed public land.

### Five core program areas

1. **Federal Lands Partnerships** — Build cooperative agreements with BLM field offices; develop MOUs with USFS ranger districts; serve as the recognized national partner for disc golf on federal land
2. **Course Development** — Identify, propose, fund, design, and volunteer-build disc golf courses on BLM and USFS land using EXPLORE Act authorities (Sections 341, 351, 153)
3. **Policy & Advocacy** — Participate in FICOR, NEPA comment processes, RMP revisions, and legislative development; join Outdoor Alliance and ORR; advocate for disc golf inclusion in recreation inventories (Section 112)
4. **Stewardship & Conservation** — Create an "Adopt-a-Course" federal lands stewardship program; develop environmental best practices for BLM terrain types; align with Leave No Trace principles
5. **Accessibility, Youth & Veterans** — Leverage Title II provisions for adaptive disc golf, youth programming, and veteran wellness partnerships

### Affiliate model (not competition)

Existing state-level disc golf nonprofits become **affiliates** — maintaining their own 501(c)(3) status, governance, and local programs while aligning on federal advocacy. ## PART 6: BLM FIELD OFFICE ENGAGEMENT PACKAGE

### Design philosophy

The best packet is **not a glossy brochure. It is a decision-support package.** Every packet should have two layers:

**Layer 1: Universal core**
- 2-page memo explaining how the proposal aligns with the EXPLORE Act
- 1-page "why disc golf fits BLM" brief
- Standard stewardship and maintenance pledge
- Standard accessibility framework
- Standard youth/veteran/community-benefit sheet
- Standard funding/match template

**Layer 2: Office-specific appendix**
- Proposed site map and photos
- Parcel/location context
- Compatibility screen for nearby uses and likely conflicts
- Basic concept design and phased infrastructure list
- Letters of support
- Named local champion
- The specific ask for that office

**The packet should always make one clear ask, not five.** Examples:
- "Please meet with us to screen this site as an underutilized developed recreation node."
- "Please evaluate whether this concept could fit your accessible-recreation planning."
- "Please advise whether this organized event should begin with RAPTOR pre-consultation."
- "Please identify what land-use, NEPA, cultural, or operational issues we should address before a formal proposal."

### Component 1: Executive advocacy one-pager

**DISC GOLF ON BLM LAND: A LOW-COST, ACCESSIBLE, STEWARDSHIP-READY RECREATION OPPORTUNITY**
*Aligned with the EXPLORE Act (P.L. 118-234)*

**What the EXPLORE Act enables:**
The EXPLORE Act creates new authorities for recreation infrastructure development on BLM land. Section 112 mandates identification of underutilized lands suitable for new recreation. Sections 214-215 require selection of new accessible recreation opportunities in each BLM region — including "any other" recreation opportunities identified with stakeholders. Section 351 (Good Neighbor Authority) allows counties to carry out recreation infrastructure improvements. Section 341 extends volunteer authority to BLM for facility construction and maintenance, with no volunteer liability insurance requirement. Section 312 expands categorical exclusions for recreation permits. Section 156 provides grants for urban-adjacent recreation in underserved communities.

**Why disc golf is an ideal fit for BLM land:**
Disc golf is a low-impact, dispersed recreation activity requiring minimal ground disturbance. An 18-hole course occupies 15-25 acres with only tee pads, metal basket targets, signage, and existing natural terrain — no irrigation, no chemical treatment, no mowing. The sport has 11,165 U.S. courses and 21.2 million annual rounds. 89% of courses are free. Average round: 93 minutes outdoors, 3,800 steps, 400 calories. BLM already recognizes disc golf as a recreation activity and manages courses at Three Peaks/Ironside (UT) and Stewart Pond (OR).

**How disc golf advances BLM's EXPLORE Act priorities:**
- **Accessibility:** Adaptable for shorter loops, accessible tees/surfaces, wheelchair-friendly layouts, adaptive clinics
- **Youth and veterans:** Low-cost, low-barrier, scalable for clinics and wellness programming
- **Underutilized sites:** Activates developed recreation nodes and day-use areas with minimal additional infrastructure
- **Shoulder-season visitation:** Year-round activity that extends recreation use beyond peak months
- **Volunteer stewardship:** Construction and maintenance are overwhelmingly volunteer-driven nationally

**The cost case:**
A 9-hole course costs $9,000-$18,000 to build. Daily capacity: 432 player-slots. Annual maintenance: $1,000-$5,000 (volunteer labor). Creekside DGC (Salt Lake City, UT) delivered 59,142 recreation-hours on ~$200K investment. Individual consumer surplus: ~$68 per trip (peer-reviewed).

### Component 3: Talking points for common BLM concerns

**"What about erosion and vegetation damage?"**
Disc golf's environmental impacts — soil compaction at tees and around baskets, vegetation trampling — are concentrated and manageable. Standard mitigation: concrete tee pads, dual pin placements, mulching, seasonal modifications (Stewart Pond reduces to 9 holes during wet conditions). No irrigation, no fertilizers/pesticides, no mowing. 97% of disc golfers will modify behavior to offset impacts (peer-reviewed).

**"What about wildlife disturbance?"**
Research documents displacement (not elimination) in the immediate area. Disc golf is quiet, non-motorized, with no projectile hazards, chemical use, or loud equipment. Seasonal closures are standard (temporary basket removal during nesting). Bear Mountain DGC (CO) was designed as wildfire prevention infrastructure.

**"Who maintains it? We don't have staff."**
The model is explicitly designed so BLM provides zero ongoing maintenance labor. The partner commits to multi-year volunteer maintenance (4-8 work days/year). Metal baskets last 10-15 years. Concrete tee pads are permanent. No mowing, watering, or chemicals. Stewart Pond has operated under volunteer maintenance since 2016. Section 341 provides statutory authority for BLM volunteer programs.

**"Is this consistent with our land use plan?"**
We will only propose sites within areas designated for recreation (SRMAs or ERMAs where recreation is authorized). If not currently designated, we will work with the field office to identify alternatives or determine whether amendment is appropriate. The Act itself requires compatibility with applicable plans.

**"What about user conflicts?"**
Disc golf is compatible with multi-use areas. At Stewart Pond, disc golfers share space with hikers, picnickers, and birdwatchers. BLM's 2025 parking management specifically addressed equitable access. Course design routes fairways away from primary trails. Low noise profile minimizes conflict.

**"What's the liability exposure?"**
Federal government is self-insured on federal land. Disc golf carries inherently low injury risk. Partner carries general liability naming BLM as additional insured. Under Section 341, volunteers are covered by the Federal Tort Claims Act with no personal insurance required. PDGA events carry tour insurance.

**"We've never done disc golf."**
BLM already manages three disc golf courses and categorizes disc golf as a recognized recreation activity in its Visit system. 11,165 U.S. courses, 21.2 million annual rounds. Same principle as ball golf but with flying discs and metal basket targets. Minimal infrastructure. We can arrange a site visit or demonstration.

## PART 7: ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY — HOW TO GET PACKETS INTO BLM OFFICES

### Sequence

**1. Pilot one office first.**
Utah is the strongest pilot state because BLM Utah was the first state used in the accessibility-page rollout, and Cedar City Field Office already publicly features disc golf at Three Peaks. Start where credibility already exists.

**2. Use a local champion, not a cold mail blast.**
Each packet should go in through a person who lives in that field office's orbit: local club leader, chamber ally, county recreation official, adaptive-sports partner, or veterans contact.

**3. Request a meeting, not an approval.**
For events, use RAPTOR pre-consultation or direct office contact. For site proposals, ask for a screening/scoping meeting with the recreation planner. BLM explicitly encourages early coordination. Do not advertise, collect fees, or begin operations until written authorization is in hand.

**4. Revise the packet after office feedback.**
Staff will often reveal the real blockers: wildlife timing, shooting conflicts, access, grazing, cultural issues, parking, admin capacity. That information goes into version two.

**5. Publish case studies.**
Once one office engages, turn that into a public case study. Awareness spreads faster through demonstrated success than abstract education.

### How to identify candidate BLM sites

1. Use **BLM Navigator** (navigator.blm.gov) to identify BLM-managed parcels within 30 minutes of your community
2. Look for: existing road access, gentle to moderate terrain, existing recreation designations (SRMA or ERMA), proximity to population centers, absence of WSA/ACEC/T&E designations
3. Cross-reference with **UDisc Disc Golf Health Index** to identify underserved areas
4. Prioritize sites near gateway communities (Section 131) or underserved urban areas (Section 156)
5. Use **PAD-US** data (USGS Protected Areas Database) to verify surface management agency
6. Visit candidate sites; photograph terrain, vegetation, access points; note existing recreation use

### How to request a field office meeting

1. Identify the correct field office at blm.gov/office/[state]
2. Request meeting with the **Outdoor Recreation Planner** or **Recreation Program Lead**
3. Frame as informational: "We'd like to discuss partnership opportunities for recreation development under the EXPLORE Act"
4. Bring the executive one-pager and a UDisc map showing disc golf coverage (or gaps) in that jurisdiction
5. Ask about: Current RMP designations, existing partnerships, Challenge Cost Share priorities, recreation inventory plans under Section 112, accessible recreation planning under Sections 214-215
6. Follow up within 48 hours with thank-you email restating discussion points and next steps
7. Request to be added to stakeholder/partner notification lists

## PART 8: PUBLIC MESSAGING

### What works

The public story should **not** be "force BLM to build courses." It should be:

- Low-cost outdoor access
- Family-friendly recreation close to communities
- Adaptive and accessible potential
- Youth and veteran wellness
- Volunteer stewardship and maintenance
- Better use of existing developed recreation nodes
- Complementing — not displacing — other recreation and conservation values

That message is much closer to the EXPLORE Act than a sport-first message, and it travels better with BLM offices, counties, chambers, and foundations.

Launch through disc golf media (UDisc, PDGA, Disc Golf Network) and social media. Pair with western landscape imagery and disc golf baskets — visually claiming the public lands identity the way Access Fund's photography claims rock faces.
