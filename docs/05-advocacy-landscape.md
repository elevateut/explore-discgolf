# Advocacy Landscape: The Gap and Models to Emulate

## The disc golf federal advocacy gap

**No disc golf entity participates in federal recreation policy.** The current situation is analogous to where climbing was in 1985-1990 — before Access Fund existed.

- **No federal agency relationships.** No MOU, cooperative agreement, or formal partnership exists between any disc golf organization and BLM, USFS, or NPS — versus IMBA's 25-year BLM cooperative agreement and TU's $8.9 million BLM partnership.
- **No D.C. policy presence.** No disc golf voice participated in shaping the EXPLORE Act, comments on BLM Resource Management Plans, or NEPA review processes.
- **No coalition membership.** Disc golf is invisible in the Outdoor Alliance and ORR networks that shape federal recreation policy.
- **No standardized process.** Unlike IMBA's trail guidelines used by BLM managers, no guidebook exists for developing disc golf courses on federal land.
- **Wrong tax structure at national level.** PDGA's 501(c)(4) status limits foundation grant eligibility and cannot receive tax-deductible charitable donations.
- **State organizations work only with municipalities.** ElevateUT Disc Golf — excellent at city/county engagement — does not engage with BLM field offices despite Utah being ~65% federal land.

The **PDGA** (founded 1976, Appling, Georgia) is a 501(c)(4) focused on sport governance — rules, ratings, tournament sanctioning. It operates the International Disc Golf Center on 975 acres. Its Environmental Committee runs the Throw Green Initiative. The **Disc Golf Foundation** is a separate 501(c)(3) focused on college courses (Matching Baskets Program), having installed 17 campus courses. Neither organization engages in federal lands policy.

No disc golf manufacturer — Innova, Discraft, Dynamic Discs, MVP, Latitude 64, Discmania — has any visible involvement in federal recreation policy.

## Model: IMBA (mountain biking)

The **International Mountain Bicycling Association** (founded 1988, Boulder, CO) provides the clearest organizational model. Born from crisis — California clubs facing trail closures — IMBA immediately offered solutions rather than confrontation, codifying its "Six Rules of the Trail."

**Federal partnership timeline:**
- **1994:** First 5-year MOU with U.S. Forest Service (191 million acres)
- **2000:** Cooperative agreement with BLM — now spanning 25+ years
- **2006:** Helped develop National Trail Classification System, classifying mountain biking as "generally acceptable on all trail classes" — a massive classification victory
- **2010:** Public Lands Initiative protected ~3,000 miles of trails, reduced wilderness-threatened trails by ~90%
- **EXPLORE Act:** Section 121 (BOLT Act) passed as a direct result of IMBA advocacy

**Operating model:** 501(c)(3), ~$7 million annual revenue (current $20 million "Leading with Trails" capital campaign), ~248 chapters through IMBA Local. Chapter model: $15/member/year flat fee, $200 setup fee, providing EveryAction CRM, group tax exemption, insurance (Nicholas Hill Group), grant access, and advocacy platform. Founding member of Outdoor Alliance.

## Model: Access Fund (climbing)

The **Access Fund** (founded 1991, Boulder, CO) is the most direct precedent for a single-sport federal advocacy win. Created because climbing areas were being closed by land managers who didn't understand the sport.

**Crowning achievement:** Section 122 of the EXPLORE Act — the PARC Act — codifying climbing as an appropriate use of wilderness areas and permanently protecting 50,000+ existing routes in 28 states. This took decades: building **150+ local climbing organizations (LCOs)**, maintaining DC policy staff, deploying economic data ($12 billion climbing industry), and mobilizing rapid threat responses (e.g., NPS's 2023 attempt to reclassify fixed anchors).

**Operating model:** ~$5-7 million annually from membership, industry partnerships (REI, Patagonia), and the Climbing Conservation Grant Program ($1,000-$4,000 grants to LCOs).

**Key lesson:** 60% of U.S. climbing areas are on federal land, which forced Access Fund into federal advocacy. Only ~0.03% of BLM land has disc golf — but the opportunity to expand is vastly larger than the current footprint.

## Model: Trout Unlimited

**Trout Unlimited** (founded 1959) demonstrates the chapter model at maximum scale: ~400 chapters, ~300,000 members, ~$50-100 million annual budget, and **$8.9 million in BLM partnership agreements** for western watershed restoration. Revenue grew from $2 million with 55,000 members (1992) to approaching $100 million — primarily through federal grant leverage, not membership alone.

## Model: Backcountry Hunters & Anglers

**Backcountry Hunters & Anglers** (founded 2004) demonstrates rapid growth: from 7 founders around an Oregon campfire to **40,000+ members** across 48 states. Success came from tapping public lands passion with a deliberately **nonpartisan identity** (1/3 Democrat, 1/3 Republican, 1/3 Independent) — a demographic profile that closely mirrors disc golf's player base.

## The coalition ecosystem

**Outdoor Alliance:** 10 member organizations (IMBA, Access Fund, American Whitewater, Winter Wildlands Alliance, American Alpine Club, Colorado Mountain Club, American Canoe Association, The Mountaineers, Surfrider Foundation) collectively representing 250,000+ committed advocates. In 2025, OA conducted **323 meetings with lawmakers** and fielded a Grasstops Collective Congressional fly-in. **Disc golf is not represented.**

**Outdoor Recreation Roundtable:** 80+ members representing businesses, trade associations, and state outdoor recreation offices. ORR led the EXPLORE Act to passage and deployed its signature stat: the outdoor recreation economy generates **$1.3 trillion and supports 5.2 million jobs**. President Jessica Wahl Turner met directly with Secretary Burgum at ORR's September 2025 summit. **Disc golf is not represented.**
